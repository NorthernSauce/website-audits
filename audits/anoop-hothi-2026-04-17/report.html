Your ideal client — a busy founder who knows they need video to grow — lands on a site with strong proof, real numbers, and clear pricing if they dig for it. The reason they still might not enquire is that your homepage tries to speak to three very different buyers at once, so nobody feels it was written for them.
A quick note on what this covers. This is a look at your strategy, messaging and content — how the site reads, what it asks visitors to do, and whether it turns a curious visitor into a booked call. Design is its own conversation and one that's much better had on a call, where I can ask about your clients, your goals and what feels right for you. If you'd like to have that chat, I'd genuinely love to.
Top 5 fixes
1
Your homepage is trying to talk to three different people — none of them feel spoken to directlyBigger project
Within the first two scrolls, you're talking to founder-led small businesses, then creative agencies, then sport brands. Each of those people has different problems, different budgets and different decisions to make. A small business owner who arrives sees agency work and thinks 'this person is probably too big for me'; an agency producer sees SME content and thinks the opposite.
Do this: Pick the one audience you most want to win, rewrite the homepage entirely for them, and move the other two to dedicated sub-pages linked from the nav.
2
Your pricing exists and is genuinely clear — but a first-time visitor has no idea until they clickQuick win
Your Pricing page (once you find it) does exactly the right thing — £400/day, £800/day, £600-2,000 retainers, all spelled out. The problem is a visitor on the homepage sees none of this, and if they don't click, they assume 'probably expensive' and move on. That's avoidable.
Do this: Put a 'from £400/day' or 'packages from £600' signal directly on the homepage so visitors self-qualify before they reach for the back button.
3
There's no way to book you without picking up the phone or filling in a form — and plenty of people won't do either at 9pm on a TuesdayQuick win
Every call-to-action across the site is 'Call Anoop' or a contact form. A founder watching your showreel at 9pm who'd happily book a 20-minute intro call right now can't, because there's no booking tool. They tell themselves they'll do it tomorrow. Most of them won't.
Do this: Add a Calendly (or similar) link to every main call-to-action — 'Book a 20-minute call' — so a visitor who's ready can commit to a time without waiting for you to call them back.
4
You have no independent reviews online — for a personal service, that's a real trust gapQuick win
A cautious buyer about to spend £800+ on someone they've never met will search your name. Right now that returns your LinkedIn, Facebook and Instagram — no Google Business Profile reviews, no Trustpilot, nothing they can verify independently. Your on-site testimonials are excellent, but a prospect doesn't know you didn't hand-pick them.
Do this: Set up a Google Business Profile and message Rai, Aliraza and Neil asking if they'd leave a short review — three real Google reviews is enough to start.
5
Visitors who aren't ready to call you today have no way to stay in your orbitBigger project
Someone lands on your site, likes what they see, but isn't ready — maybe they've got a shoot in 6 months, or they're still building their budget. Right now they leave and forget about you. There's nothing for them to take away, no email list, no guide, no reason to come back.
Do this: Create one simple lead magnet — a short PDF like 'The 5 videos every small business should have on their website' — and offer it in exchange for an email address on the homepage.
Detailed findings
Why aren't visitors getting in touch?

Why aren't visitors getting in touch?

2 / 7  ·  29% ▼
This checks every point of friction between a visitor who's interested and one who's actually made contact. One unclear button, one extra step, one 'we'll get back to you' without a timeframe — and they're gone.
PARTIAL
One clear next step on every section
You've got 'Call Anoop' buttons, a contact form, WhatsApp, and 'Book a Call' links all on the same page. Every option on its own is fine — stacked together, visitors get decision fatigue and pick nothing.
Fix: Pick one primary action per section (ideally a booking link) and demote the others to secondary text.
PARTIAL
Getting in touch is genuinely easy
You've got a working contact form and WhatsApp, which is solid. What's missing is a booking tool so someone ready to commit to a time can do it immediately rather than waiting for you to call back.
Fix: Add Calendly or a similar booking link so visitors can self-book a 20-minute intro call.
PASS
3 clicks or fewer from landing to enquiring
Your contact form is on the homepage itself, and there's a direct Contact page in the nav. A visitor who wants to get in touch can do it in one click.
FAIL
Main call to action AND a lower-commitment option
Every action on your site asks someone to start a conversation with you. There's nothing for the 80% of visitors who like what they see but aren't ready to talk yet — no guide, no email list, no way to stay connected.
Fix: Add one simple lead magnet (a short PDF or video) that someone can download in exchange for their email.
PASS
Clear what happens after someone gets in touch
Your contact page spells it out well — fill in the form and you'll call them, and it even tells them what to share (goal, dates, budget) so they're prepared. That removes real uncertainty.
PARTIAL
Navigation set up for first-time visitors
Your nav is clean and logical — Home, Work, Pricing, Contact. The small cart icon in the top-left (a Squarespace default) implies you sell something when you don't, which can briefly confuse first-time visitors.
Fix: Hide the cart icon from your Squarespace settings so the nav isn't suggesting a shop that doesn't exist.
PARTIAL
The page builds a case from start to finish
Your homepage moves through problem → testimonial → case studies → process → contact, which is a solid structure. The disjointedness comes from jumping between three audiences (see Standing Out), which breaks the narrative flow.

Why would anyone pick you over everyone else?

Your homepage speaks to SME founders, creative agencies AND sport brands — three very different buyers with very different problems. A specialist picks one and owns it; serving three makes you look like a generalist who takes whatever work comes in.

PARTIAL
Why this business over the alternatives
Your 9 years of experience and cross-sector credibility (NatWest, Heinz, TikTok, Microsoft) are genuinely strong. What's missing is the explicit comparison — why you over a cheaper local freelancer, or a production company.
FAIL
Honest about challenges and limitations
There's no 'who this isn't right for' anywhere on the site. Businesses that only publish positives signal they'll say yes to anyone — which makes premium buyers hesitate.
Fix: Add a short 'who I'm not the right fit for' section on the About page (e.g. 'budgets under £X', 'one-off family events').
PASS
Reflects current business
The case studies reference 2025 shoots, the pricing is published, the Events offer is specific and current. The site feels actively maintained.
