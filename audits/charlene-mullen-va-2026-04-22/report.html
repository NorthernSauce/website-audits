Your ideal client will land on this site, immediately understand what you do, and feel like you get their problem — but most will hold back from getting in touch because there's nothing yet to prove it's worked for someone else like them.
Everything in this report is about strategy, messaging, and content — how well the site speaks to the right person and gives them a reason to get in touch. It doesn't cover design; getting that right needs a proper understanding of your business and clients, and that's a conversation worth having on a call.
Top 5 fixes
1
Someone ready to book has nowhere obvious to go — so they don'tQuick win
The only way to get in touch is to send an email from scratch, which asks a lot of someone who's already stretched thin. A business owner who's been buried in emails all day is the last person who wants to write a considered introduction to a stranger — they want to pick a time, click it, and know it's done.
Do this: Add a free Calendly booking link to the Contact page (and ideally the homepage too) so someone ready to act can book a 15-minute chat in one click.
2
There's no proof it's worked for anyone — so a nervous client won't risk itQuick win
Handing over access to your inbox is a big step for any business owner, and the first thing they'll look for before saying yes is evidence that someone else took that leap and was glad they did. Right now there's nothing — no names, no results, no reassurance anywhere — and that gap is doing real damage to your enquiry rate.
Do this: Ask your first client for a short quote with their name and what changed for them — even one specific testimonial on the homepage does more for trust than anything else you could add.
3
Most visitors will say 'I'll think about it' — and never come backQuick win
Every page on the site assumes the visitor will return when they're ready. But someone who's drowning in admin will visit, nod along, and disappear straight back into their inbox — because there's nothing that makes the status quo feel uncomfortable enough to act today rather than later.
Do this: Add a line about how many clients you take on at a time — 'I keep my client list small so I can do things properly; get in touch to check if I have space' — it creates gentle urgency without feeling pushy.
4
Your best credential is buried on the About page where most people won't see itQuick win
A decade managing complaints at ombudsman level — in banking and energy — is an unusual and genuinely impressive background for someone handling inboxes. It tells a potential client that you can deal with anything, stay calm under pressure, and communicate well. But it's nowhere near the homepage where it would actually influence a decision.
Do this: Pull one line from your About page story onto the homepage — something like 'I spent 10 years managing inboxes in banking; yours won't faze me' — positioned near the main call to action.
5
A visitor who isn't quite ready has nothing to read — so they leaveBigger project
Many of your ideal clients will land on the site genuinely interested but not ready to hand over inbox access to someone they've just found. Without any articles, guides, or FAQs, there's nothing to keep them engaged or build trust while they make up their mind — and they'll leave without giving you a second chance.
Do this: Write three short articles answering the questions your ideal client is Googling: 'how do I know if I need a VA?', 'what can a VA actually take off my plate?', and 'how much does a VA cost?' — these bring the right people in and warm them up before they ever contact you.

Fix: Add a free booking link (Calendly or equivalent) so someone can pick a time and confirm in one click, without having to compose an introductory email first.

There's nothing on the site that tells a visitor why they should choose you over the dozens of other VAs offering inbox and admin support. Remove your name and logo and this site could belong to almost anyone in the category.
Fix: Your banking and complaint-management background is unusual and genuinely credible — bring that front and centre on the homepage rather than leaving it buried on the About page.
FAIL
Specialist, not generalist
The services page covers email management, inbox detox, social media, community management, and operational support — a wide range that positions you as a generalist. Generalists compete on price; specialists get chosen because they feel like the right fit.
Fix: Consider leading with one or two signature services you're known for, even if you offer more — it makes it much easier for the right client to say 'that's exactly what I need.'
PASS
Offer clarity
The pricing page is excellent — clear rates, transparent package prices, and a good explanation of the different options. Most VA sites avoid this entirely; yours removes a friction layer before the visitor even has to ask.
PARTIAL
Why this business over the alternatives
The 'What is a VA?' section makes a strong case for using a VA over hiring an employee, with a specific and useful cost comparison. But it doesn't address why a potential client should choose you specifically over another VA doing similar work.
Fix: Add a short line or section that speaks to what makes working with you different — your background, your approach, or the type of client you're particularly good at helping.
FAIL
Honest about challenges and limitations
The site doesn't mention who this service isn't right for. Businesses that only show positives can feel like they're trying to be everything to everyone, which makes it harder to trust them with something as personal as inbox access.
Fix: Add a short line — on the services or pricing page — about who you work best with and who might be better served elsewhere.
PASS

FAIL
There's a reason to act now, not later

Fix: Add a short line about your current availability — 'I take on a small number of clients at a time; get in touch to check if I have space' — to create gentle urgency without pressure.
