Your ideal client — a B2B service business owner who's sick of looking like everyone else — will read your homepage and feel found within five seconds, which is unusually rare in this category. Where you lose them is the trust check at the moment of decision: anonymous testimonials, case studies with no numbers, and no external reviews to back any of it up — a careful buyer doing one last bit of homework on you walks away.
Quick note before you dig in — this audit looks at your strategy, messaging, and content. It doesn't grade the design. Design matters, but it needs context about your business, your clients, and what you're trying to pull off — and that's a conversation best had on a call rather than in a written report. Everything below is about whether your site is doing the sales job it could be doing, not what it looks like.
Top 5 fixes
1
Your testimonials don't say who said them — so they barely count as proofQuick win
The two quotes on your homepage say things any prospect would love to hear, but there's no name, no business, no photo, and no date attached to either. To a buyer who's careful with their money, that reads as 'this could be made up' — and the goodwill the rest of your site has built quietly drains away.
Do this: Pick your three happiest clients, ask each for a one-line quote with their full name, business name, and a headshot, and put them on the homepage with the photo right next to the quote.
2
Your case studies tell the story but skip the numbers — and numbers are what closeBigger project
Duke's Plumbing case study has a great quote about people no longer haggling, but no actual figures — no leads-per-month, no revenue change, no traffic data. The buyer reading it wants to picture themselves in those numbers, and without them, the story is just a feeling.
Do this: Go back to two or three of your best clients and ask for one specific metric each — enquiries up, revenue up, ad spend down — and put it as a big number at the top of every case study.
3
There's no path for someone who's interested but not ready to bookQuick win
Every button on your site sends visitors to the same place — book a call. A serious buyer doing research isn't ready for that on visit one, so they bookmark the site and very often never come back. You're missing the warm middle ground.
Do this: Add one low-commitment option — a downloadable checklist, a 'questions to ask any web designer' guide, or an email signup tied to your insights — so visitors can engage without picking up the phone.
4
Your Insights section last published in 2021 — it's actively making you look inactiveBigger project
All five blog posts are from 2020 and 2021. A prospect doing due diligence on you opens the Insights tab, sees the dates, and quietly downgrades you in their head — even though the rest of the site says you're sharp and current. A dead blog is worse than no blog.
Do this: Either commit to publishing one new piece per quarter starting now, or remove the Insights link from the nav and lean into the FAQ as your content asset instead.
5
There are no reviews anywhere a buyer would actually look for themBigger project
We searched for you on Google reviews, Trustpilot and ProductReview.com.au and found nothing. Word-of-mouth referrals will absolutely Google you before they enquire — and the silence at that moment costs you the ones who play it safe.
Do this: Set up your Google Business Profile this week, then send the five clients you're closest with a one-line message asking for a review with the direct link.
