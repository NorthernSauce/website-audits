A doctor landing here would sense this is the right specialist, but leave without enquiring — because there's no pricing, no real content to build trust, and the copy keeps talking about the business instead of the doctor's situation.
This audit looks at strategy, messaging, and content — the things that determine whether a visitor becomes an enquiry. Design is a whole separate conversation, and one that's much more meaningful once we understand your clients, your goals, and what you're trying to achieve. That's a call worth having.
Top 5 fixes
1
Remove the misleading logo stripQuick win
The NHS, GMC, and RCGP logos signal client relationships — but these are regulatory bodies every UK doctor belongs to, not organisations you've worked with. A doctor who recognises this will immediately question everything else on the page.
Do this: Remove those logos entirely, or replace them with logos of real clients who've given permission to be named.
2
Add a Calendly booking linkQuick win
Your contact form requires a multi-step exchange before anything is confirmed — someone fills it in, then waits to hear back, then arranges a time. That's three points of drop-off before a call ever happens.
Do this: Add a direct Calendly link alongside the form so a doctor can book a slot in 30 seconds without waiting for a reply.
3
Add pricing contextQuick win
There is no mention of cost anywhere on the site — not a 'from' figure, not a pricing model, nothing. For a busy doctor who's already wary of accountancy fees, silence on price reads as 'probably expensive' and they move on.
Do this: Add a short paragraph explaining how you price — whether that's a monthly retainer, an annual fee, or a 'typically starts at' figure — so the right people self-qualify instead of bouncing.
4
Rewrite the copy so the doctor is the subject, not the businessQuick win
Almost every major section opens with 'we', 'our', or 'at DrAccountant' — the business is the hero of its own website. A doctor reading this feels like a prospect being sold to, not someone who's found the right partner.
Do this: Go section by section and flip the first sentence of each so it starts with 'you' — 'You get an accountant who actually knows what an NHS pension looks like from the inside' lands harder than 'We specialise in NHS pension advice'.
5
Replace agency-written content with one real article and one real case studyBigger project
Every blog post and every case study on the site is published under Visuable's name — the web agency that built the site. A prospect who clicks through will see 'Written by Visuable' and an agency bio, which signals this content was never really from you.
Do this: Write one article as Dr. Othman — pick a real GP tax mistake you've seen repeatedly — and rewrite the £30,000 case study in the first person, removing Visuable's authorship.

Strategy

Strategy

2 / 7  ·  29% ▼
PASS
Specialist, not generalist
The site is unambiguously positioned for healthcare professionals only, and the GP-founded angle is prominent. This survives the name-removal test — strip the logo and it still couldn't belong to a general accountancy firm.
PASS
Clear differentiator
Founded by a practising GP who personally navigated NHS pension and tax complexity is a differentiator that can't be faked or easily copied. It's specific, credible, and immediately meaningful to a doctor.
PARTIAL
Customer is the hero, not the business
The homepage opens with a doctor-focused metaphor, which is a good start, but quickly shifts to 'we specialize', 'we offer', 'we understand' — the business becomes the main character within two paragraphs.
Fix: Rewrite each homepage section so the first sentence is about the doctor's situation, not the business's capabilities.
PARTIAL
Clear villain identified
NHS pension complexity and juggling multiple income streams are referenced, but they're listed as features of the service rather than named as the root cause of real stress a doctor feels. The villain is implied but never given a face.
Fix: Pick one villain — 'the NHS pension is the most complex financial decision a doctor makes, and most get it wrong' — and name it explicitly above the fold.
PARTIAL
Reflects current business
Blog posts and case studies are dated Dec 2024 (one shows a 2019 placeholder date) and are all authored by the web agency that built the site. The content doesn't reflect the business as it exists today.
Fix: Replace agency-authored content with at least one article and one case study written by the founders.
FAIL
Offer clarity
The services page names Tax Planning and Bookkeeping & Payroll but gives no detail on what's included, how the engagement works, or what it costs. A doctor can't understand what they'd actually be buying without making contact first.
Fix: Add a brief scope note under each service explaining what's included and how delivery works, plus a pricing anchor.
FAIL
Direct CTA + transitional CTA both present
Every single CTA on the site is 'Book your Free Consultation' — there is no lower-commitment option for a doctor who's interested but not ready to book. Visitors who aren't ready to commit have nowhere to go.
Fix: Add one transitional option — a downloadable guide on NHS pension decisions, or a newsletter signup — so there's a path for warm visitors who aren't ready to book yet.
Messaging

Messaging

2 / 8  ·  25% ▼
PASS
Guide empathy expressed
The About page origin story is genuine — Dr. Othman took a sabbatical to learn finance after experiencing the exact stress the target client feels. That's real empathy, not a marketing claim.
PASS
Guide authority demonstrated
Dr. Othman holds MSc, PhD, and MRCGP credentials alongside firsthand GP experience; Lucas holds MAAT with 15 years in healthcare accounting. Both are visible and credible.
PARTIAL
Hero headline names the client's specific situation or problem
The headline 'Caring for your Accounts, Like you care for Patients' is memorable and passes the 5-second comprehension test — you know what they do in seconds. But it names the category, not the problem; a GP reading it knows this is an accountant for doctors, but doesn't feel found.
Fix: Test a version that names the stress: 'Your NHS pension is one of the most complex financial decisions you'll make — and most doctors get it wrong without specialist help.'
PARTIAL
Internal problem acknowledged
The emotional layer — the stress of financial complexity while running a demanding medical career — surfaces on the About page but not prominently on the homepage where it would do the most work.
Fix: Move one sentence about the emotional reality of being a doctor managing finances into the hero section of the homepage.
PARTIAL
Clear plan visible
The 'How it works' section has three steps, but step 2 is 'Let Us Work Our Magic' — this tells a cautious doctor nothing about what actually happens with their money and their data.
Fix: Replace the vague middle step with something concrete: 'We review your income streams, pension position, and tax exposure, then build a plan tailored to your career stage.'
PARTIAL
Success picture painted
Peace of mind, clarity, and confidence appear throughout, but they stay abstract. The transformed life after working with DrAccountant is never made concrete enough to feel real.
Fix: Add one specific outcome sentence — 'Know exactly what your NHS pension will pay out before you make a single career decision that affects it.'
FAIL
Philosophical stakes implied
There's no sense of the bigger 'why it matters' — no acknowledgement that financial mistakes cost doctors years of security, or that getting this right enables the career and life they've worked for.
Fix: Add one sentence about what's really at stake: getting NHS pension decisions wrong isn't a financial inconvenience, it's a retirement problem that takes decades to play out.
FAIL
Cost of inaction present
Nothing on the site suggests what happens to a doctor who doesn't sort this out — no mention of overpaying tax, pension penalties, missed allowances, or HMRC exposure. Without stakes, there's no urgency.
Fix: Add one line to the hero section about what's silently at risk: 'Most doctors overpay tax by thousands every year simply because their accountant doesn't know how NHS income works.'
Copywriting

Copywriting

1 / 7  ·  14% ▼
PASS
5-second hero test
Within 5 seconds it's clear: specialist accountancy for doctors, run by a GP and an accountant, book a free consultation. The medical metaphor headline and tagline work together for fast comprehension — though note this is a different test from whether the headline resonates as a problem statement (it doesn't — see Messaging).
PARTIAL
Services explain outcomes, not just names
Tax Planning gets partway there — 'minimizes tax liabilities' is a result, but it stops short of the emotional end. 'Knowing exactly what you'll owe before HMRC asks' is more visceral for a doctor who's been caught out before.
Fix: Add one outcome sentence per service that describes what life looks like after the problem is solved, not just what the service does.
PARTIAL
Long copy genuinely scannable
The Squarespace layout creates visual spacing, but the 'Why us' section has six blocks of paragraph text in succession — a doctor scanning on their phone will give up before reaching the end.
Fix: Reduce each 'Why us' block to two lines max, or convert the section to a simple icon-plus-one-line format.
FAIL
Copy leads with client, not business
A quick count of the homepage shows 'we', 'our', and 'at DrAccountant' opening most major sections. The services page opens: 'Our healthcare accountants are experts in medical accounting services' — the business is the subject almost everywhere.
Fix: Audit every section heading and opening line: if it starts with 'we' or 'our', rewrite it so the doctor is the subject.
FAIL
Headlines earn the scroll
Section headings across the site — 'Tailored Accountancy Solutions', 'Why us', 'How it works', 'Get in touch' — are labels, not hooks. None of them give a reason to read what follows.
Fix: Rewrite at least the three most-read section headings so they pull the visitor forward: 'Why most doctors overpay — and how to stop' beats 'Why us' every time.
FAIL
No weasel words or filler
'Tailored' appears at least eight times across the site; 'comprehensive', 'seamless', 'personalized', and 'dedicated' are scattered throughout. These are non-falsifiable claims that could appear on any accountancy website in the country.
Fix: Do a find-and-replace on 'tailored', 'comprehensive', and 'personalized' — every instance should be replaced with a specific claim or deleted entirely.
FAIL
Pricing addressed somewhere
There is no mention of cost, fees, or pricing structure anywhere on the site — not a 'from' figure, not a pricing philosophy, nothing. For a doctor weighing up whether to enquire, this is the single biggest friction point.
Fix: Add a pricing section or a short paragraph on the services page explaining how you charge — monthly retainer, annual fee, or a starting-from figure — so the right people self-qualify.
