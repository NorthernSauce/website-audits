A GP landing here would sense they've found the right specialist — but leave without enquiring, because the site never makes them feel understood, hides the price, and gives no easy way to book.
This audit looks at strategy, messaging, and content — the things that determine whether a visitor becomes an enquiry. Design is a whole separate conversation, and one that's much more meaningful once we understand your clients, your goals, and what you're trying to achieve. That's a call worth having.
Top 5 fixes
1
A doctor who's ready to book can't — so they don'tQuick win
A GP finishes reading your site at 9pm between patients. They're interested. They hit Contact, fill in the form, and wait. By the time you reply, they've either forgotten or found someone else who had a booking link. The form creates a gap between 'I'm interested' and 'I've booked' — and that gap is where most enquiries disappear.
Do this: Add a Calendly link directly on the contact page and in the main call to action so someone can book a 20-minute call in 30 seconds without waiting for a reply.
2
Silence on price sends doctors straight to GoogleQuick win
A doctor landing here for the first time — probably already wary of accountancy fees — will look for a price before doing anything else. When there isn't one, they don't ask. They assume expensive and move on to someone willing to give them a ballpark. You don't need a price list, just enough for the right people to self-select.
Do this: Add one short paragraph explaining how you charge — whether that's a monthly retainer, an annual fee, or a 'typically starts at' figure — so the right people stay and the wrong ones don't waste your time.
3
The site talks about you when it should be talking to themQuick win
Almost every section of the homepage opens with 'we', 'our', or 'at DrAccountant' — the business is the main character of its own website. A GP reading this feels like they're being pitched to rather than understood, and people don't hire businesses that pitch at them; they hire ones that seem to already get their situation.
Do this: Go section by section and flip the opening sentence of each so it starts with 'you' — 'You get an accountant who knows what an NHS pension looks like from the inside' lands harder than 'We specialise in NHS pension advice'.
4
Three blog posts and a 2019 date signal the site was set up and never touchedBigger project
A prospect who clicks your blog to check your expertise finds a post dated March 2019 with a web address ending in random letters — the platform default from the day the site launched. It's a small thing, but to a doctor deciding who to trust with their finances, it signals that no one has looked at this in years, which is the opposite of the reassurance they need before picking up the phone.
Do this: Publish one real article written by Dr. Othman on a GP tax issue that doctors actually search for (and give it a proper web address), then update the case study addresses from /case-study-2 to something matching the title.
Detailed findings
Strategy

Strategy

2 / 7  ·  29% ▼
Is the site built around a clear story and a defined audience? The goal is for the right person to land on this site and immediately feel like they've found the right place — not a generic business that serves everyone.
PASS
Specialist, not generalist
The site is unambiguously positioned for healthcare professionals only, and the GP-founded angle is prominent. This survives the name-removal test — strip the logo and it still couldn't belong to a general accountancy firm.
PASS
Clear differentiator
Founded by a practising GP who personally navigated NHS pension and tax complexity is a differentiator that can't be faked or easily copied. It's specific, credible, and immediately meaningful to a doctor.
PARTIAL
Site puts the client first, not the business
The homepage opens with a doctor-focused metaphor, which is a good start, but shifts to 'we specialize', 'we offer', 'we understand' within two paragraphs — the business becomes the main character. When visitors read about you instead of recognising their own situation, they disengage; people only stay engaged with content that feels like it's about them.
Fix: Rewrite each homepage section so the first sentence is about the doctor's situation, not the business's capabilities.
PARTIAL
The core problem is named clearly
NHS pension complexity and multiple income streams are mentioned, but as features of the service rather than the real source of stress a doctor carries. Without naming the core problem clearly there's no tension — and without tension there's no reason for a visitor to feel they need to do something about it.
Fix: Name the core problem directly — 'the NHS pension is the most complex financial decision a doctor makes, and most get it wrong' — and put it front and centre above the fold.
PARTIAL
Reflects current business
Blog posts are dated December 2024 — with one showing a 2019 placeholder date that was never updated — and the content reads like it was written at launch and never revisited. A doctor who notices this will wonder whether the business is still actively engaged with its clients, or just coasting.
Fix: Replace agency-authored content with at least one article and one case study written by the founders.
FAIL
Offer clarity
The services page names Tax Planning and Bookkeeping & Payroll but gives no detail on what's included, how the engagement works, or what it costs. If a doctor has to contact you just to understand what they'd be buying, most won't bother — they'll go to a competitor who answers that question upfront.
Fix: Add a brief scope note under each service explaining what's included and how delivery works, plus a pricing anchor.
FAIL
Main call to action and a lower-commitment option both present
Every CTA on the site pushes straight to booking a consultation, with nothing for a doctor who's interested but not ready to commit yet. Most visitors won't book on a first visit — without a lower-stakes option like a guide or newsletter, those warm leads leave with no way to bring them back.
Fix: Add one transitional option — a downloadable resource on NHS pension decisions, or a newsletter signup — so there's a path for warm visitors who aren't ready to book yet.
Messaging

Messaging

2 / 8  ·  25% ▼
Does the site speak to the right person about the right problem? Good messaging makes the ideal client feel understood before they're sold to. If the copy could belong to any business in the same category, it's not doing its job.
PASS
Genuine understanding of the client's situation shown
The About page origin story is genuine — Dr. Othman took a sabbatical to learn finance after experiencing the exact stress the target client feels. That's real empathy, not a marketing claim.
PASS
Credibility and expertise clearly evidenced
Dr. Othman holds MSc, PhD, and MRCGP credentials alongside firsthand GP experience; Lucas holds MAAT with 15 years in healthcare accounting. Both are visible and credible.
PARTIAL
Opening headline connects to the client's desire or situation
The headline 'Caring for your Accounts, Like you care for Patients' is clever and memorable — you understand what they do within seconds. But it names the service category rather than the deeper desire a doctor actually has: financial security, the freedom to focus on patients, or knowing retirement is sorted — the things that make getting the finances right feel worth doing.
Fix: Test a headline that names what the doctor actually wants from life, not what DrAccountant does: something like 'Focus on patients. We'll make sure the money works.' or 'The financial clarity that lets you practice medicine without the weight of the numbers.'
PARTIAL
The emotional frustration behind the practical problem is addressed
The real emotional driver — the stress of managing finances while running an already demanding medical career — is buried on the About page rather than leading the homepage. People act on emotional problems, not logical ones; moving this to where the decision is actually made changes the entire conversion dynamic.
Fix: Move one sentence about the emotional reality of being a doctor managing finances into the opening section of the homepage.
PARTIAL
Clear plan visible
The 'How it works' section has three steps, but step 2 is 'Let Us Work Our Magic' — which tells a cautious doctor nothing about what actually happens to their money, their data, or their time. A vague process feels like a risk; a clear one feels like a decision you can make confidently.
Fix: Replace the vague middle step with something concrete: 'We review your income streams, pension position, and tax exposure, then build a plan tailored to your career stage.'
PARTIAL
Success picture painted
Peace of mind, clarity, and confidence appear throughout, but they're abstract — every accountancy firm says something similar. Specific outcomes ('know exactly what your NHS pension will pay out before you make a career decision that affects it') create genuine desire in a way that vague promises never can.
Fix: Add one specific outcome sentence — 'Know exactly what your NHS pension will pay out before you make a single career decision that affects it.'
FAIL
The bigger picture — what's really at stake — comes through
There's no sense of the bigger picture — no acknowledgement that financial mistakes cost doctors years of retirement security, or that getting this right is what enables the career and life they've worked two decades to build. Without that framing, hiring an accountant feels like a chore; with it, it feels like one of the most important decisions they'll make.
Fix: Add one sentence about what's really at stake: getting NHS pension decisions wrong isn't a financial inconvenience, it's a retirement problem that takes decades to play out.
FAIL
There's a reason to act now, not later
Nothing on the site suggests what a doctor is silently losing by not having this sorted — no mention of overpaying tax, pension penalties, or missed allowances that can't be recovered after the fact. Without a reason to act now rather than later, most visitors will bookmark the site and never come back.
Fix: Add one line to the opening section about what's silently at risk: 'Most doctors overpay tax by thousands every year simply because their accountant doesn't know how NHS income works.'
Copywriting

Copywriting

1 / 7  ·  14% ▼
Is the writing actually moving a visitor toward making contact? Every line should earn its place. If it doesn't bring the reader closer to getting in touch, it shouldn't be there.
PASS
Clear within 5 seconds what you do and who for
Within 5 seconds it's clear: specialist accountancy for doctors, run by a GP and an accountant, book a free consultation. The headline and tagline work together for fast comprehension — though whether it makes the right doctor feel found is a separate question, covered in Messaging.
PARTIAL
Services explain outcomes, not just names
Tax Planning gets partway there — 'minimizes tax liabilities' is a result — but it stops short of something a busy doctor can actually feel. People buy outcomes, not service names; 'knowing exactly what you'll owe before HMRC asks' lands differently than 'we minimise your tax liabilities.'
Fix: Add one outcome sentence per service that describes what life looks like after the problem is solved, not just what the service does.
PARTIAL
Long copy genuinely scannable
The Squarespace layout helps with spacing, but the 'Why us' section has six blocks of paragraph text in a row — a doctor scanning on their phone between patients will give up before reaching the end. Content that doesn't get read doesn't do any work.
Fix: Reduce each 'Why us' block to two lines max, or convert the section to a simple icon-plus-one-line format.
FAIL
Copy leads with client, not business
A count of the homepage shows 'we', 'our', and 'at DrAccountant' opening most major sections — the services page alone opens with 'Our healthcare accountants are experts.' When the business is the subject of almost every sentence, a visitor reads it as a sales pitch rather than a service built around them.
Fix: Audit every section heading and opening line: if it starts with 'we' or 'our', rewrite it so the doctor is the subject.
FAIL
Headlines earn the scroll
Section headings across the site — 'Tailored Accountancy Solutions', 'Why us', 'How it works' — describe what's coming rather than giving a reason to read it. A heading that hints at something useful pulls the reader forward; a label just marks territory.
Fix: Rewrite at least the three most-read section headings so they pull the visitor forward: 'Why most doctors overpay — and how to stop' beats 'Why us' every time.
FAIL
No weasel words or filler
'Tailored' appears repeatedly throughout the site — in headlines, service descriptions, and the 'why us' section — alongside 'comprehensive', 'seamless', and 'dedicated'. Every accountancy website in the country uses these words, which means they give a visitor no reason to choose this one over any other.
Fix: Do a find-and-replace on 'tailored', 'comprehensive', and 'personalized' — every instance should be replaced with a specific claim or deleted entirely.
FAIL
Pricing addressed somewhere
There is no mention of cost, fees, or pricing structure anywhere — not a 'from' figure, not even an explanation of how you charge. A doctor who can't get a rough sense of the investment will either assume the worst and leave, or contact you just to ask the price — which is the most avoidable friction in the whole enquiry process.
Fix: Add a pricing section or a short paragraph on the services page explaining how you charge — monthly retainer, annual fee, or a starting-from figure — so the right people self-qualify.

User Journey

User Journey

2 / 6  ·  33% ▼
Can a visitor get from landing on the site to making contact without friction? This checks whether the path to an enquiry is clear, short, and easy — and what happens once someone decides they want to get in touch.
PASS
One clear next step on every page
'Book your Free Consultation Today' is the single, consistent call to action across every page — one obvious next step everywhere. Giving visitors multiple CTAs at once splits their attention and reduces the chance they act on any of them, so this is exactly right.
PASS
3-click path to enquiry
Homepage to contact form is a single click, with the CTA button in the nav and repeated throughout every page. Every extra step between interest and action loses a percentage of visitors — this removes almost all of that friction.
PARTIAL
Homepage tells a logical story
The homepage introduces the business before establishing any problem or tension — 'who we are' comes before a visitor has been given a reason to care. A visitor who doesn't feel the problem first won't feel the value of the solution, so leading with the business rather than the client's situation costs you the emotional connection that drives action.
Fix: Move the 'why us' points earlier and lead with the client's situation before introducing the business.
PARTIAL
Contact/booking genuinely low-friction
The contact form requires five fields, a reCAPTCHA, and then a wait for a manual reply before anything is confirmed. That gap between clicking 'get in touch' and having a call actually booked is where most enquiries fall apart — a direct booking link removes it entirely.
Fix: Add a Calendly link alongside the form — see Fixes — so instant booking is possible.
PARTIAL
Navigation serves new visitors, not existing clients
The nav has a redundant Home link (the logo already does this) and lists Resources before Contact — which buries the most important action behind content most first-time visitors won't want first. Nav order signals priority; anything that pushes Contact further right reduces the chance it gets clicked.
Fix: Remove the Home link and move Contact before Resources in the nav order.
FAIL
Clear next step after enquiry
The contact page gives no indication of what happens after you submit — how quickly you'll hear back, what the first call looks like, or that there's no obligation attached. Uncertainty at the exact moment of action is one of the most common reasons people don't follow through, even after they've decided they want to.
Fix: Add three lines below the form: when you'll hear back, what the first call covers, and that there's no obligation.
