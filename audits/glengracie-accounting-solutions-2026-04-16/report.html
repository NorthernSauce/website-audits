Your site has a strong visual presence and genuine personality — but the copy talks about what you do rather than what your clients get, and there's nothing to convert the visitor who's interested but not yet ready to ask for a quote.
Top 5 priority fixes
1
Rewrite the hero to make the right person think "that's me"
"Reassuring, relatable, responsible accountancy" is a nice line but it doesn't speak to a specific situation. A visitor who's frustrated with an absent accountant, or confused about their finances, won't see themselves in it. The first line of your site needs to do that job.
Action: Replace the hero headline with something that names the problem your ideal client is experiencing — for example: "Fed up chasing your accountant? We actually pick up the phone." One sentence that makes the right person stop scrolling.
2
Add case studies with real, specific results
Your testimonials are genuine and attributed, which is great — but none of them show a measurable outcome. "She helped me understand my figures" is warm but vague. A short case study with context, a problem, and a concrete result would be far more persuasive.
Action: Ask one or two clients if you can write up their story. Keep it short: what was the situation when they came to you, what did you do, and what changed? Even one case study with a specific result ("saved £3,400 in overpaid tax in year one") would outperform ten glowing-but-vague reviews.
3
Replace "Get a quote" with an outcome-focused CTA
"Get a quote" puts the pressure on immediately — it implies commitment before a visitor knows if you're even the right fit. It also tells them nothing about what actually happens when they click. This friction costs you enquiries from people who are interested but not quite ready.
Action: Change your primary CTA to something like "Book a free 20-minute chat" or "See if we're a good fit." It sets expectations, removes pressure, and is far easier to say yes to than "get a quote."
4
Add something for visitors who aren't ready to enquire yet
Right now, someone who visits your site and isn't quite ready to get in touch has only one option: leave. There's no newsletter, no free guide, no reason to stay connected. Those warm leads are lost forever and will likely end up with a competitor when they're ready.
Action: Add a simple low-commitment option — a short guide on something your clients always ask about (e.g. "5 things to sort before your year-end"), a newsletter, or even a free tax health check. Something that keeps them in your world until the timing is right.
5
Give visitors some idea of your pricing
Hiding price entirely means visitors can't self-qualify. People who might be perfect clients leave because they assume you're out of their budget — and people who'd push back on your fees waste your time with enquiries that don't go anywhere. Either way, you lose.
Action: Add a short pricing section — it doesn't have to be exact. Even "Most of our small business clients invest between £X and £Y per month" gives people enough context to decide whether to take the next step.
