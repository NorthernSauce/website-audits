Your site looks professional and the technical content is thorough — but staging domain errors may be stopping Google from showing you in search results at all, the contact page lists email addresses that might not reach you, and there's almost no social proof despite working with Formula 1 teams and global companies.
Top 5 priority fixes
1
Fix the staging domain contamination — it may be hiding you from Google
Your site's page metadata is pointing to a staging address (kenworthengineering-co-uk.stackstaging.com) rather than your live domain. Your contact page also shows email addresses and links using this staging address — anyone trying to email you directly from that page may never reach you. There's also a "noindex" instruction in your site's code that tells Google not to show you in search results. This looks like a leftover from when the site was being built that was never cleaned up. It could mean your site is effectively invisible to people searching for you.
Action: Ask whoever built your site — or a developer — to check your WordPress settings for any references to the staging domain, update all internal URLs to your live domain, and remove the "noindex" tag. Also test your contact form to confirm enquiries are actually landing in your inbox.
2
Add client testimonials and show the companies you work with
Your site claims to work with "some of the world's largest companies" across automotive, F1, oil & gas, renewable energy and more — but there isn't a single client name, logo, quote, or outcome anywhere on the site. For a B2B precision engineering business where trust is everything, this is a significant gap. Prospects making big decisions about who machines their parts need proof before they pick up the phone.
Action: Gather 3–5 testimonials from existing clients, ideally with their name, company, and a specific outcome (delivery time hit, tolerance achieved, problem solved). Add at least one client logo strip to the homepage. Even a short case study — "we machined X for Y company, here's what was involved and what they said" — would do more work than anything else on the site.
3
Put a real person on the About page
Your About page is a short paragraph about the company's history and capabilities. There's no mention of Natalie or the family behind the business, no photos of the team, and no personal story. For a family-run business with 30+ years of history, that's a missed opportunity — people buy from people, and "family-owned" is a differentiator that needs a face and a story to land.
Action: Rewrite the About page around the real story — who started it, why, what drives the business today, who the customer is talking to when they call. Add a photo of the team on the shop floor or the person they'll speak to when they enquire. This doesn't need to be polished — authentic beats professional here.
4
Rewrite the copy to lead with what clients get, not what you do
Every page on your site — including the hero — talks about Kenworth's capabilities, machines, and processes. The visitor already knows they need CNC parts. What they're trying to work out is whether you're the right supplier. Copy that says "we manufacture parts to sub-10 micron tolerance for the world's most demanding industries" is less compelling than something that speaks to what that means for the client: right first time, on time, no rejected batches, no delays to your production line.
Action: Rewrite the hero subheading and the opening of each service page to lead with what the client gets — the outcome, the reassurance, the problem solved — before explaining how you do it. The machine specs and capabilities can stay, just move them further down the page.
5
Deal with the stale blog — update it or remove it
Your News & Insights section shows three posts, the most recent from April 2023 — three years ago. A blog that hasn't been touched in three years signals an inactive business to anyone who spots it, and it's likely doing more harm than good.
Action: Either remove the News & Insights section from the main navigation and quietly archive it, or commit to publishing something quarterly — even a short update about a project, a new machine, or an industry sector you've been working in. If you're not going to maintain it, removing it is the better option.
