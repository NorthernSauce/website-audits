The verdict
The site looks professional on the surface, but it tells visitors what you are rather than why they should care — and every "Learn More" button leads nowhere, leaving potential clients with nowhere to go.
Top 5 Priority Fixes
1
Fix or remove every "Learn More" button — they all go nowhere
Every "Learn More" link on the page points back to the top of the same page. A visitor who clicks to find out more about your service levels or management accounts gets taken straight back to the headline — it's a dead end that wastes their time and signals the site is unfinished.
Action: Either link each "Learn More" to a specific section further down the page using anchor links (e.g. #management-accounts), or create dedicated service pages. If neither is possible right now, remove the buttons entirely — a missing link is better than a broken one.
2
Rewrite the hero to name a real problem, not just your job title
"Accountants for small businesses" tells visitors what you are — it doesn't make anyone feel understood or compelled to read on. The clients who need you most are stressed about tax, unsure if they're being paid what they're owed, or losing sleep over whether their numbers add up. Lead with that, not your category.
Action: Replace the main headline with something that names the specific situation your best clients are in when they find you. For example: "Stop losing sleep over tax and start focusing on the business you're actually building" — then let the subheadline explain what you do and where you're based.
3
Add an About section — people hire people, not logos
There's nothing on the site that tells visitors who Jack Sinclair actually is. Your MAAT qualification, why you started the business, what kind of clients you work best with — all of this is missing. For a sole trader firm, the person behind it is the biggest trust signal you have.
Action: Add an About section to the homepage (or a full About page) with a real photo of Jack, a short personal story, your MAAT qualification, and who you do your best work with. Keep it warm and human — not a CV. This alone will separate you from 80% of accountant websites.
4
Give your testimonials names and real results — anonymous quotes don't convert
You have three testimonials on the site and they're better than average — one mentions appealing penalties, another mentions stress about tax. But none have a name, business, or photo attached. Anonymous quotes carry almost no weight because visitors can't verify them and they feel interchangeable with any other website.
Action: Go back to the three people who gave those testimonials and ask permission to use their first name, business name, and ideally a headshot or company logo. If they're happy, update each quote with full attribution. A named, specific testimonial is worth ten anonymous ones.
5
Add a booking tool — making people email or call to enquire loses leads
You already offer a free 15-minute consultation, which is a great idea. But to book it, a visitor has to pick up the phone or send an email manually. That's a meaningful barrier — many people who are interested but not yet ready to speak to someone will leave rather than take that step.
Action: Add a free Calendly link (calendly.com) so visitors can book a consultation slot directly from the website. Link it from the "Book a free 15 minute consultation" CTA at the bottom of the page. This takes under an hour to set up and typically increases enquiry rates immediately.

Whether Google Analytics or a similar tracking tool is installed cannot be confirmed from the site content. Many website builder platforms include basic traffic data in their dashboards, but this may not give enough insight into visitor behaviour, drop-off points, or which pages convert.
