The verdict
Your ideal client — a small service business owner stressed about tax and cash flow — would land here, struggle to find any reason to choose Jack over any other local accountant, and leave without enquiring: there's no booking tool, no photo of Jack, no credentials visible, and nothing in the headline that makes them feel understood.
Top 5 Priority Fixes
1
Add a booking tool — you're losing people who won't pick up the phone
The CTA says "Book a free 15 minute consultation today" but clicking through shows a phone number and email address. Someone who's ready to enquire right now won't remember to call later — they'll close the tab and move on to the next accountant whose website lets them book in 30 seconds.
Action: Sign up for a free Calendly account (calendly.com), set up a 15-minute slot, and replace the phone/email contact with a Calendly link. This takes under an hour and typically produces an immediate uplift in enquiries.
2
Replace the stock handshake photo — it signals "generic accountant" before a word is read
The hero image is a close-up of two suited men shaking hands — it's used by hundreds of accountancy websites across the UK. Before your ideal client has read a single word, the site already feels interchangeable with every other firm they could find on Google.
Action: Replace it with a real photo of Jack — working at his desk, on a client call, or a professional headshot. Any authentic image of the actual person doing the work builds more trust than the best stock photo you can buy.
3
Rewrite the hero headline to name your client's situation, not your job title
"Accountants for small businesses" tells visitors what you are — it doesn't make anyone feel understood or compelled to read on. Your ideal client is stressed about what they owe HMRC, unsure if their numbers are right, or trying to grow without losing control of their cash flow. Lead with that.
Action: Replace the main headline with something that names a real situation — for example: "Stop stressing about tax and start running your business again." Then use the subheadline to say who you are and where you're based.
4
Add names to those testimonials — anonymous quotes carry almost no weight
You have three testimonials and the content is genuinely good — one mentions appealing penalties, another mentions stress about tax, which are exactly the outcomes your ideal client cares about. But without a name, business name, or photo, they look like they could have been written by anyone.
Action: Contact those three clients and ask permission to use their first name and business name alongside their quote. If they're happy, update the testimonials. One named, specific testimonial beats five anonymous ones.
5
Add an About section with Jack's photo and qualifications
Your ideal client is trusting you with their money and their business compliance — but there's no photo of Jack, no qualification listed (MAAT, AAT, ACCA, or similar), and no personal story anywhere on the site. For a sole-trader firm, the person behind it is the biggest trust signal you have, and right now it's invisible.
Action: Add a short About section to the homepage: a real photo of Jack, two or three sentences on why he started the business and who he does his best work with, and the qualification he holds. Warm and human — not a CV.
