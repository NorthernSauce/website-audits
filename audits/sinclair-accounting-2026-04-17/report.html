You're clearly set up as an accountant for small businesses — but visitors are leaving without getting in touch because every question they arrived with (how much does it cost, who have you helped, why you over anyone else) goes completely unanswered.
This report looks at whether your site is set up to turn visitors into enquiries — the strategy, messaging, and content that make someone decide to get in touch. Design matters too, but judging it properly needs a conversation about your business, your clients, and your goals. That's one for a call.
Top 5 fixes
1
Every 'Learn More' button skips to your booking form before visitors are readyQuick win
A business owner scrolling through your services sees 'Learn More' and clicks expecting detail — instead, they're sent straight to 'Book a free 15-minute consultation.' That's asking for a commitment before they understand what they're buying. Most visitors who aren't ready to book will just leave rather than scroll back up to keep reading.
Do this: Update each Learn More button to expand or link to the relevant service description — a visitor who fully understands what you offer is far more likely to book a call than one who's still figuring it out.
2
There's no way to get in touch without picking up the phone or opening an email appQuick win
The only contact options are a phone number and an email address — nothing someone can fill in at 10pm when they're thinking about their finances. On mobile, tapping an email address opens an app most people don't have set up, and many visitors will abandon rather than switch apps. Every extra step between 'I want to get in touch' and 'I've got in touch' costs you enquiries.
Do this: Add a Calendly link so someone can book a 15-minute call directly from the site without needing their phone or email app — it takes under an hour to set up and immediately removes the biggest friction point.
3
There's no mention of cost anywhere — so visitors assume the worst and leaveQuick win
A small business owner weighing up whether they can afford an accountant will look for a price before they do anything else. When they can't find one, they don't call to ask — they assume expensive and move on to someone willing to give them a ballpark. You don't need a full price list; you just need enough for the right people to decide it's worth getting in touch.
Do this: Add one sentence explaining how you charge — even 'monthly retainers for small businesses typically start at £X' removes the biggest reason people leave without making contact.
4
There's nothing online to confirm you're real and trustworthy beyond the site itselfQuick win
Anyone who's interested will Google you before they call. Right now there's nothing from anyone other than you saying you're worth hiring — no Google reviews, no independent listings, nothing. The three testimonials on the site have no names or photos, which makes them nearly impossible for a stranger to take seriously.
Do this: Set up a Google Business Profile and ask your two or three best clients to leave a review — even five real Google reviews transforms how you appear to someone comparing accountants in Chester-le-Street.
5
Visitors don't know who they'd actually be working withQuick win
There's no section introducing you — no photo on the homepage, no story, and the AAT qualification that appears in the browser tab description isn't visible anywhere on the page itself. For a service this personal — someone with full access to your finances — people need to feel they know something about who they're calling before they'll pick up the phone.
Do this: Add a short section with a real photo, two or three sentences about your background and why you work with small businesses, and your AAT qualification clearly visible — a photo next to a short paragraph is enough.

Why aren't visitors getting in touch?

Why aren't visitors getting in touch?

1 / 8  ·  12% ▼
This checks every point of friction between a visitor who's interested and one who's actually made contact. One unclear button, one extra step, one 'we'll get back to you' without a timeframe — and they're gone.
FAIL
Main call to action AND a lower-commitment option both present
The only way to take action is to call or email — there's no lower-commitment option for someone who's interested but not quite ready to pick up the phone. Visitors who aren't ready to act yet have no reason to stay connected.
Fix: Add one lower-commitment option — a free guide, a short email series, or a 'subscribe for tax tips' — so the people who aren't ready to call yet have a reason to stay in touch.
FAIL
What happens next is explained clearly
There's no explanation of the process — what happens after someone gets in touch, what the first conversation looks like, how quickly they'd hear back. A visitor who doesn't know what they're committing to when they click 'Book a call' is less likely to do it.
Fix: Add three simple steps — 'Book a free 15-min call → We review your situation → You get a clear plan, in plain English' — it removes the uncertainty that stops people from making contact.
PARTIAL
One clear next step on every page
The main call to action — 'Book a free 15-minute consultation' — is clear at the bottom. But the five 'Learn More' buttons that appear throughout the services section all scroll directly to the booking form rather than providing more information, which pushes visitors toward a commitment before they've had the chance to fully understand what they're buying.
Fix: Link the Learn More buttons to the relevant service detail rather than the booking form — let visitors educate themselves before you ask them to act.
PARTIAL
Homepage builds a case from start to finish
The structure — intro, services, detailed services, testimonials, contact — is broadly logical, but the site leads with services before showing any understanding of the client's situation. A visitor who doesn't feel understood in the first two sections won't read as far as the testimonials.
Fix: Move the empathy before the sell — open with the client's situation, then introduce your services as the solution.
PASS
3 clicks or fewer from homepage to submitting an enquiry
On a single-page site, getting in touch requires just one action — tapping the phone number or email address. The path to contact is short.
FAIL
Getting in touch is genuinely easy
Phone and email are the only contact options — there's no form and no booking tool. On mobile, tapping an email address opens a mail app many people don't use, and there's no fallback. Visitors who want to get in touch outside of office hours or without a phone call have no good option.
Fix: Add a Calendly booking link or a simple contact form so someone can take action directly from the site without needing any other app.
PARTIAL
Navigation is set up for first-time visitors, not existing clients
There's no page navigation — a visitor who wants to jump straight to testimonials or the contact section has to scroll the entire page to find them. On a mobile screen this is a meaningful friction point.
Fix: Add a simple sticky nav or anchor links to the main sections so visitors can find what they need without scrolling through everything first.
FAIL
It's clear what happens after someone gets in touch
There's no indication of how quickly someone will hear back or what the first conversation will actually look like. A visitor who doesn't know what they're committing to when they click 'Book a call' is less likely to follow through.
Fix: Add a one-line response promise and a brief description of the first call — 'We'll reply within one working day; our first chat is a free 15 minutes to see if we're a good fit' removes the last reason not to get in touch.

Does your site speak to the right person?

Does your site speak to the right person?

0 / 8  ·  0% ▼
People don't buy services. They buy solutions to problems they feel. This checks whether your site opens in the client's world — names the problem they actually lose sleep over, speaks to the frustration underneath it, and shows them what life looks like once it's solved.
PARTIAL
The site puts the client first, not the business
There is some client-focused language — 'letting you focus on what you do best', 'you're running a small business and need practical help' — but most major sections open with 'At Sinclair Accounting, we...' which puts the business at the centre. A visitor landing here doesn't immediately feel 'this site gets me'; they feel 'this site is about them.'
Fix: Rewrite section openers so they start with the client's situation — 'You're running a business and tax is the last thing you want to think about' opens in their world, not yours.
PARTIAL
The core problem the client faces is named clearly
The site mentions 'stress of taxes' and 'simplifying financial information' — the problem is present but surface-level. The specific, felt fear that actually drives a small business owner to find an accountant — an unexpected tax bill, cash flow drying up, getting it wrong and facing penalties — isn't named anywhere.
Fix: Name one concrete problem in the opening section — 'Not knowing what you owe until HMRC tells you' is something your ideal client actually worries about.
FAIL
Opening headline connects to the client's desire or situation
'Accountants for small businesses' is a category label, and 'Your Partner in Financial Growth' describes the business relationship rather than what a client actually wants. Neither makes the right person think 'that's exactly me' or pulls them toward the outcome they're after — more money in their pocket, less time worrying about tax, a business they feel in control of.
Fix: Rewrite the headline to speak to the desire your ideal client has — something like 'Run your business. We'll make sure HMRC isn't a problem.' gives them a reason to keep reading.
FAIL
The emotional frustration behind the practical problem is addressed
The site mentions 'stress of taxes' but never goes deeper into what that stress feels like — the anxiety every time an unexpected bill arrives, the feeling of being out of control of your own money, the worry that you might be getting it wrong. Without naming the feeling, the site stays on the surface of why someone actually picks up the phone.
Fix: Add one sentence that shows you understand the feeling underneath the problem — 'Most of our clients came to us after a tax bill they weren't expecting' does more work than any list of services.
FAIL
The bigger picture — what's really at stake — comes through
There's no sense of what having the right accountant actually makes possible for a small business owner beyond 'financial growth' — a vague phrase that slides off the page. What does a financially sorted business actually give you? The confidence to hire? The freedom to take on more work? A business you could eventually sell?
Fix: Add one line about what this really makes possible — not 'growth' but the specific thing your best clients tell you their life looks like now.
FAIL
The site shows it understands the client's situation before it sells anything
The site moves straight into listing services without first showing it understands what a small business owner is dealing with day to day. Visitors feel sold to rather than understood, and people don't buy from businesses that pitch before they connect.
Fix: Open with one or two sentences that describe the client's world before you mention a single service — show you get their situation before you offer to fix it.
PARTIAL
What life looks like after working with this business is described
'Letting you focus on what you do best' and 'unbelievable growth' in the testimonials hint at the outcome, but they're vague enough that a visitor can't picture what their business actually looks like once they're working with you.
Fix: Get specific: 'Close your laptop on Friday knowing exactly what you owe, what's coming in, and what you can pay yourself' gives your ideal client something real to want.
FAIL
There's a reason to act now, not later
Nothing on the site makes the status quo feel uncomfortable — no sense of what's being lost every month without proper financial management, no urgency, no reason today is better than next month. A visitor on the fence will bookmark the page and never come back.
Fix: Add one honest line that makes waiting feel costly — 'Every month without a clear view of your finances is a month you might be overpaying tax or missing a growth opportunity' is specific and true.
